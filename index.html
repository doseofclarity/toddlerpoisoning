Toddler Poisoning Like You Are 5 Years Old

🧸 Imagine you have a little brother or sister who just learned to walk and climb.

• Little kids are like scientists – they put everything in their mouths to explore!
• Sometimes, they find colorful pills (like candy) or shiny bottles under the sink.
• If they eat or drink something harmful, it can make them sick – like a bad tummy ache, sleepiness, or trouble breathing.

🩺 But don't worry! Doctors and nurses know special ways to help – like giving medicine to stop the poison or using a special black powder (activated charcoal) to soak it up.

✅ Best of all, grown‑ups can prevent almost all poisonings by putting dangerous things in a high locked cabinet.

Why toddlers are most at risk

High curiosity
Short stature – reaches counters
Puts hands in mouth constantly

Definition & Epidemiology

Toddler poisoning refers to accidental exposure to a potentially harmful substance (ingestion, inhalation, skin contact, or eye contact) in a child aged 1–3 years. It is a major cause of pediatric injury and is almost entirely preventable.

📊 Global Statistics
• Children aged 1–3 years are the most likely age group to be poisoned.
• Peak incidence of pediatric poisonings occurs in toddlers age 1 to 3 years, as does the peak incidence for hospitalization.
• Non‑fatal poisoning is more common in the age group 1 to 4 years old.
(Source: BMJ Best Practice, WHO EMRO)
🇵🇭 Philippines
• Poisoning is one of the leading causes of injury among children below 18 years of age.
• Most pediatric admissions for poisoning were below 5 years old, occurring at home and accidental in nature.
• In Metro Manila, poisonings accounted for 0.17% of pediatric admissions (3 hospitals, 1996‑2000).
(Source: Philippine National Injury Survey, DOH AO 2006‑0016; HERDIN)
⚠️ Key point: Most poisonings occur in the child's own home, often when a parent or caregiver is nearby but distracted. Poisoning in children is almost entirely preventable with appropriate safety measures.

Common Poisons in Toddlers

💊 Medications (most common – about half of all toxic exposures)
• Paracetamol (acetaminophen)
• Ibuprofen
• Multivitamins (iron containing most dangerous)
• Oral contraceptives
• Antiepileptic drugs
• Cough and cold preparations
🧴 Household products
• Cleaning agents (bleach, toilet bowl cleaners)
• Dishwashing liquid / laundry detergent pods
• Pesticides
• Hydrocarbons (gasoline, kerosene, lamp oil)
• Button batteries (burns in 2 hours)
• Lead (paint chips, soil, imported toys)
📌 Highly toxic in small amounts: Paracetamol (toxic dose >150 mg/kg), opioids (e.g., methadone), calcium channel blockers, clonidine, sulfonylureas, camphor, and hydrocarbon fuels.

Pathophysiology & Toxidromes

🔬 General Mechanisms
• Absorption: enteral (most common), inhalation, dermal
• Distribution: protein binding, lipid solubility
• Metabolism: liver (cytochrome P450 system)
• Excretion: kidneys (urinary pH manipulation)
• End‑organ toxicity: hepatotoxic (paracetamol), nephrotoxic, neurotoxic (lead)
🧩 Common Toxidromes (predictable symptom clusters)
• Cholinergic (organophosphates): SLUDGE (salivation, lacrimation, urination, defecation, GI upset, emesis)
• Anticholinergic: “hot as a hare, blind as a bat, dry as a bone, red as a beet, mad as a hatter”
• Sympathomimetic: tachycardia, hypertension, hyperthermia, agitation
• Opioid: miosis, respiratory depression, coma
🔑 Nursing pearl: Toxidromes help identify the likely poison before laboratory confirmation.

Clinical Features & Assessment

👶 Cardinal signs
• Witnessed ingestion or child found with empty container
• Sudden change in behaviour (drowsiness, irritability, seizures)
• Gastrointestinal symptoms: vomiting, abdominal pain, diarrhoea
• Respiratory: tachypnoea, bradypnoea, stridor, apnea
• Burns around mouth (caustics)
• Odor on breath (gasoline, alcohol)
🩺 Diagnostic approach
• ABCDE assessment (airway, breathing, circulation, disability, exposure)
• Fingerprick glucose (exclude hypoglycaemia)
• Serum paracetamol and salicylate levels (first‑line toxicology)
• Electrocardiogram (tricyclics, calcium channel blockers)
• Chest/abdominal X‑ray (opaque tablets, button batteries)
📌 Red flags (call poison centre immediately): Altered mental status, seizures, respiratory depression, arrhythmias, refractory shock, or ingestion of a highly toxic substance (e.g., opioids, calcium channel blockers, camphor).

Management & Antidotes

⚕️ General principles (ABC + Decontamination)
• Stabilise airway, breathing, circulation (ABCs).
• Remove from source; decontaminate skin/eyes with copious water.
• Activated charcoal (oral/NG) – within 1 hour of ingestion; dose 1 g/kg (max 50‑60 g). Ineffective for hydrocarbons, caustics, alcohols, heavy metals.
• Gastric lavage rarely indicated; contraindicated in caustic/hydrocarbon ingestion.
• Enhanced elimination: multiple‑dose activated charcoal (MDAC), urinary alkalisation (bicarbonate for salicylates), haemodialysis for severe methanol/ethylene glycol toxicity.
💊 Specific antidotes
• Paracetamol → N‑acetylcysteine (NAC)
• Opioids → Naloxone (0.4‑2 mg IV titrated)
• Organophosphates → Atropine + Pralidoxime (PAM)
• Benzodiazepines → Flumazenil (avoid in chronic use)
• Methanol / Ethylene glycol → Ethanol or Fomepizole
• Beta‑blockers / Calcium channel blockers → Glucagon (0.15 mg/kg)
• Isoniazid → Pyridoxine (75 mg/kg)
📞 Pillitteri advice: “Never present medication as candy. Buy medications with childproof caps; put away immediately after use.”

Nursing Care & Prevention

👩‍⚕️ Nursing interventions
• Secure airway, administer oxygen, establish IV access.
• Obtain history (what, when, how much, interventions done).
• Call poison control centre (US: 1‑800‑222‑1222).
• Administer activated charcoal if indicated (protect airway).
• Monitor vital signs, pulse oximetry, cardiac rhythm.
• Provide emotional support to family.
🛡️ Primary prevention (parent teaching)
• Store all medicines and household chemicals in locked cabinets, out of sight and reach (preferably ≥1.5 m high).
• Use child‑resistant caps; never refer to medicine as “candy”.
• Keep products in original containers; never use food containers for chemicals.
• Remove button batteries and small magnets from reach.
• Install carbon monoxide detectors.
• Role‑play “always ask first” before touching anything.
📌 Pillitteri prevention mnemonic “S.A.F.E.” – Store locked, Avoid transferring containers, Focus (never leave child alone with medications), Educate children about poisons.

Q: Antidote for paracetamol overdose
N‑acetylcysteine (NAC)
Q: Antidote for opioid poisoning
Naloxone (0.4‑2 mg IV titrated to effect)
Q: When is activated charcoal contraindicated?
Ingestion of hydrocarbons, caustics, alcohols, heavy metals
Q: Cholinergic toxidrome treatment
Atropine + pralidoxime (organophosphates)
Q: Button battery ingestion action
Emergency! Can cause esophageal burns in 2 hours – immediate X‑ray and removal.
